A Study on the Inconsistency between Machine and Human Perceptions of Speaker Attributes

Speaker attributes, as an essential element of the information conveyed by the speech signal, can be perceived by both human auditory systems and machine algorithms. Particularly, speaker modeling algorithms provide tools for machine perception of speaker attributes. Conventionally, it is assumed that human and machine perceptions of speaker attributes are consistent and aligned with the speaker identity that produced the speech. However, the inconsistency between human and machine perceptions of speaker attributes has been observed in various studies. This paper aims to further investigate this inconsistency using generated speech, within which the speaker attributes are controllable. To this end, speech generation models that disentangle speaker information and represent it with embedding vectors are applied. Given an original speech utterance, the speaker-modified version is generated by manipulating the speaker embedding vector. Specifically, six modification methods are examined in this study, including uniform noise, Gaussian noise, speckle- noise, salt noise, subspace removal, and MI-FGSM. Comparison results obtained on the LibriSpeech dataset demonstrate that: (1) inconsistency exists between machine and human perceptions of speaker attributes, (2) with controlled modifications, the machine perception of the original speaker can be altered while the human perception is preserved. Moreover, the efficacy of the speaker modification methods in enhancing the asynchronous voice anonymization capability was validated in the experiments.
